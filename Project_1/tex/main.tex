\section*{Introduction}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Introduction}

In this project, the magnetostatic analysis is conducted on a system that consists of a simple C-core stator and a variable reluctance rotor (i.e. plunger). For this system, first the inductance and torque expressions are analytically derived. For this derivation, it is assumed that the reluctance of the system changes sinusoidally, which is a very strong assumption for such a system. After the analytical derivations, the resulting expressions are plotted and given in the report. Then, the system is modeled on a FEA software, which is selected as Ansys Maxwell 2D and the modeling procedure is explained. After the modelling, the system is simulated using a linear material without any saturation or hysteresis. The resulting flux density vectors in the system are plotted and shown for three different plunger configurations. Also, the inductance and stored energy in the system are calculated for these three configurations and presented. Additionally, the variation of inductance, stored energy and torque acting on plunger as the plunger rotates are also presented in this report. Then, the same analysis is conducted with a nonlinear material. The results of the FEA simulation is discussed, especially the the effects of the non-idealities. The FEA results are then compared with the analytical results and the possible sources of the discrepancies are pointed out. Finally, a current control method to establish continuous rotation of the plunger is proposed. The flux density animation of the FEA results are presented in the GitHub repo.

\section*{Analytical Modelling}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Analytical Modelling}

\subsection*{Inductance-Reluctance Calculations}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Inductance-Reluctance Calculations}
In this part, we find the minimum reluctance and inductance values of the system analytically. Since we assume that the core is infinitely permeable, the only contribution to the reluctance of the system comes from the air gap. Hence, the maximum reluctance occurs when the air gap cross section is exposed to the maximum air gap length (i.e. 5 mm) uniformly and minimum reluctance occurs when the air gap cross section is exposed to the minimum air gap length (i.e. 1 mm). These two rotor positions are illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:1}.
\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{Images/Fig1_final.eps}
    \caption{Rotor positions for minimum reluctance - maximum inductance and maximum reluctance - minimum inductance configurations and corresponding air gap lengths. (a) shows the minimum reluctance - maximum inductance configuration. (b) shows the maximum reluctance - minimum inductance configuration.}
    \label{fig:1}
\end{figure}

The reluctance contribution of the air gap can be calculated as follows.

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{R}=\frac{l_{gap}}{\mu_0A_{gap}}
\end{equation}

Assuming that the air gap has a rectangular cross section, we can find the cross section area of the air gap as follows. 

\begin{equation}
    A_{gap} = 15 *25*10^{-6} = 3.75*10^{-4} \; m^2
\end{equation}

So, we can find the maximum and minimum reluctance values as follows.

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{R}_{min}=\frac{1*10^{-3}}{4\pi*10^{-7}*3.75*10^{-4}}=2.12*10^6 \;At/Wb
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{R}_{max}=\frac{5*10^{-3}}{4\pi*10^{-7}*3.75*10^{-4}}=10.6*10^6 \;At/Wb
\end{equation}

We can find the inductance of the system for a given reluctance as follows.

\begin{equation}
    L=\frac{N^2}{\mathcal{R}}
\end{equation}

Where $N$ is the number of turns of the coil. Thus we can find the maximum and minimum inductance values as follows.

\begin{equation}
    L_{max}=\frac{300^2}{\mathcal{R}_{min}}=\frac{300^2}{2.12*10^6} = 42\;mH
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    L_{min}=\frac{300^2}{\mathcal{R}_{max}}=\frac{300^2}{10.6*10^6} = 8.5\;mH
\end{equation}

To calculate the variation of inductance as a function of rotor position, we may assume a sinusoidal change in the air gap length as the rotor rotates since the cross section area is not exposed to full minimum or maximum air gap lengths uniformly as the rotor rotates. Let's assume the following air gap length variation as a function of the rotor position, assuming the rotor position is $\theta$ and Figure \ref{fig:1}(a) shows $\theta=0^0$ position. 

\begin{equation}
    l_{gap}(\theta) = 3-2\cos(2\theta) \;mm
\end{equation}

This way, we obtain the minimum air gap length at $\theta=0^0$ and maximum air gap length at $\theta=90^0$. So, the reluctance becomes,

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{R}(\theta) = \frac{(3-2\cos(2\theta))*10^{-3}}{4\pi*10^{-7}*3.75*10^{-4}}=(6.37-4.24\cos(2\theta))*10^6 \;At/Wb
\end{equation}

Finally, the inductance becomes,

\begin{equation}
    L(\theta)=\frac{300^2}{(6.37-4.24\cos(2\theta))*10^6} \;H = \frac{42.5}{3-2\cos(2\theta)} \;mH
\end{equation}

Plotting the above expression, we get the inductance variation as a function of rotor position given in Figure \ref{fig:2}.

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{Images/InductanceVariation.eps}
    \caption{Inductance variation as a function of rotor position.}
    \label{fig:2}
\end{figure}

\subsection*{Torque Calculations}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Torque Calculations}

We know that the reluctance torque created by constant excitation can be calculated as follows.

\begin{equation}
    T = \frac{1}{2}I^2\frac{dL(\theta)}{d\theta}
\end{equation}

We can calculate the derivative of the inductance with respect to the rotor angle as follows.

\begin{equation}
    \frac{dL(\theta)}{d\theta}=-\frac{42.5*10^{-3}}{(3-2\cos(2\theta))^2}*4\sin(2\theta)=\frac{170*10^{-3}\sin(2\theta)}{(3-2\cos(2\theta))^2}
\end{equation}

So, the torque expression becomes, 

\begin{equation}
    T=-\frac{2.5^2}{2}\frac{170*10^{-3}\sin(2\theta)}{(3-2\cos(2\theta))^2}=-\frac{0.53}{(3-2\cos(2\theta))^2} \; Nm
\end{equation}

Plotting the torque with respect to the rotor angle, we obtain the response given in Figure \ref{fig:3}.

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{Images/TorqueVariation.eps}
    \caption{Torque variation as a function of rotor position.}
    \label{fig:3}
\end{figure}


\subsection*{Possible Model Improvements}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Possible Model Improvements}

The following model improvements can be made to improve the accuracy of the analytical calculations.

\begin{itemize}
    \item The non-homogeneous flux distribution can be modeled by taking the cross section area smaller than the the original area by some factor.
    \begin{equation}
        A_{eff} = KA_{gap}\;, \qquad K\in [0.5,1]
    \end{equation}
    \item In this part, we assumed that the core is infinitely permeable, which is not the case in a real system and the core has also a contribution to the reluctance. We can also consider this reluctance in the calculations as follows.
    \begin{equation}
        \mathcal{R}_{core} = \frac{l_{core}}{\mu_0\mu_rA_{core}}
    \end{equation}
    \begin{equation}
        \mathcal{R}=\mathcal{R}_{core}+\mathcal{R}_{gap}
    \end{equation}
    \item We can represent the effect of the fringing flux with an increase in the air gap reluctance by some fraction of the theoretical reluctance. If we assume \%10 fringing flux, then,
    \begin{equation}
        \mathcal{R}_{gap,eff}=1.1\mathcal{R}_{gap}
    \end{equation}
    \item For the leakage flux, again, we can represent the effect with an increase in the reluctance of the air gap similar to previous case.
    \item We can also represent the nonlinear behavior of the permeability by taking it as a function of excitation. In this case, the torque expression also depends on the derivative of the excitation current. 
\end{itemize}

\section*{FEA Modelling (2D - Linear Materials)}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{FEA Modelling (2D - Linear Materials)}
For the FEA simulation of the system, I used Ansys Maxwell with Student License. The simulation setup and results are presented in the following sections.

\subsection*{Geometry}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Geometry}

The following geometry given in Figure \ref{fig:4} is used to simulate the system in Ansys Maxwell.

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{Result_Image/Geom_edited.png}
    \caption{Geometry of the System.}
    \label{fig:4}
\end{figure}

In this geometry, I replicated the given geometry in the project definition. In this geometry,

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A is the cross section of the coil which has a current in the direction of +z direction.
    \item B is the cross section of the coil which has a current in the direction of -z direction (return path of the current).
    \item C is the main frame of the magnetic core.
    \item D is the ferromagnetic plunger.
    \item E is the surrounding air and the air gap.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Meshing}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Meshing}
For the meshing, I used the Maxwell curved meshing tool with the coarser setting for the meshing of everything except the air gap zone. For the air gap zone, I used a maximum mesh size of 2mm using mesh operations tool. I set the mesh refinement per pass to \%30 and minimum number of passes to 5 for adaptive mesh refinement of Ansys. The resulting mesh geometry is given in Figures \ref{fig:5} and \ref{fig:6}. Note that, since Ansys Student License allows for a maximum of 2000 cells, I could not refine the mesh further.

\subsection*{Boundary Conditions}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Boundary Conditions}
As the boundary conditions, I set regions A and B of Figure \ref{fig:4} as "coil" with 300 turns, and A in positive and B in negative directions. I set the material for these regions as "copper". Then, I created a "winding" using A and B coils and set the current as 2.5 Amperes. I set the material for regions C and D as "ferrite" with a linear permeability of $\mu_r=1000$. I created a project parameter $\theta$ as the orientation of the plunger using the "rotate" command. I set the initial $\theta = 0$ value as the the shown configuration in Figure \ref{fig:4} and created an Optimetric analysis setup to sweep the parameter $\theta$ to obtain results for different orientations of the plunger. I set the material for zone E as air and set the "Balloon" boundary condition.

\subsection*{Solver Settings}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Solver Settings}
I used the default solver settings for Ansys Maxwell 2D except for the mesh refinement settings mentioned before.

\section*{Control Method}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Control Method}
To achieve a continuous rotation with this system, we need a net torque acting on the system as it mate a full rotation. So, I proposed to use a on-off strategy on the DC current. In this case, I turned on the current on the negative torque region of the plunger (i.e. $\theta\in [0^0,90^0]\cup [180^0,270^0]$) and turned it off on the positive torque region. The resultant current and torque waveforms are given in Figure \ref{fig:20}.

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{Images/ControlledResult.eps}
    \caption{Current and Torque Variations for the Controlled Case.}
    \label{fig:20}
\end{figure}

From the result, we can see that we achieve a net torque acting on the plunger as it makes a full rotation. The torque is negative due to the sign convention I used in the FEA. From the result we can see that such a control method results in very large zero torque regions and torque ripple since we are not able to utilize half of the period to create torque. Finally, such a system has no way to self-start and it needs an initial torque for starting to rotate.
